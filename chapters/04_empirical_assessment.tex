\chapter{Empirical assessment of the control framework}
\section{Experimental protocol and evaluation setup}
\label{subsec:experimental_protocol_setup}

All numerical experiments in this thesis are performed within the same effective control framework introduced in the previous section. The physical system is modeled as a pair of weakly anharmonic superconducting modes truncated to three local levels each, so that the full Hilbert space has dimension
\(d_{\mathrm{phys}} = 3^2 = 9\),
% \begin{equation}
% d_{\mathrm{phys}} = 3^2 = 9,
% \label{eq:validation_physical_dimension}
% \end{equation}
while the computational two-qubit subspace retains dimension
\(d_{\mathrm{comp}} = 4\).
% \begin{equation}
% d_{\mathrm{comp}} = 4.
% \label{eq:validation_computational_dimension}
% \end{equation}
The dynamics are generated by the effective gmon Hamiltonian introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:gmon_effective_hamiltonian}, and are simulated in discrete time with timestep \(\Delta t\) over a finite control horizon \(T\). At each time step, the control action specifies the seven-dimensional control vector
\begin{equation}
\bm{u}(t)
=
\bigl(
g(t),\,
\delta_1(t),\delta_2(t),\,
f_1(t),f_2(t),\,
\phi_1(t),\phi_2(t)
\bigr),
\label{eq:validation_control_vector}
\end{equation}
consisting of a tunable coupling pulse, two local detuning controls, two microwave amplitudes, and two microwave phases. These controls are passed through a finite-bandwidth filter before being applied to the Hamiltonian, so that the simulated pulses remain consistent with the experimentally motivated smooth-control assumptions built into the framework. For every experiment, the target operation is taken from the gate family
\begin{equation}
N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)
=
\exp\!\left[
i\left(
\alpha\,\sigma_1^x\sigma_2^x
+
\alpha\,\sigma_1^y\sigma_2^y
+
\gamma\,\sigma_1^z\sigma_2^z
\right)
\right],
\label{eq:validation_target_family}
\end{equation}
and control quality is assessed through the same UFO cost structure introduced previously, namely a weighted combination of gate infidelity, leakage penalty, boundary penalty, and runtime. This uniform physical and numerical setup is essential for the interpretation of the experiments, because it ensures that all methods are compared within the same multilevel model, under the same control constraints, and against the same class of target gates.


\subsection{Compared methods}
Within this common framework, four optimization and comparison schemes are considered.
\begin{itemize}
    \item The first is a direct gradient-based baseline, implemented through the Adam optimizer, which searches directly over the filtered control trajectory for a fixed gate horizon.
    \item The second is a policy-gradient reinforcement-learning controller trained in the nominal environment, that is, without stochastic perturbations applied during training.
    \item The third is the noise-optimized TRPO controller, which is trained in a stochastic environment where Gaussian perturbations are injected into the control amplitudes and into the anharmonicity-related parameter entering the Hamiltonian.
    \item The fourth comparison is not a pulse-level optimizer but a circuit-level reference, namely the optimal gate-synthesis baseline used to benchmark runtime on the family \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)\).
\end{itemize}
These four methods play different methodological roles. The Adam baseline tests whether a standard first-order optimization of the raw control variables is sufficient to obtain high-quality pulses. The nominal TRPO controller tests whether reinforcement learning is already competitive in the idealized deterministic setting. The noise-optimized TRPO controller tests whether robustness can be learned directly from stochastic training. Finally, the gate-synthesis baseline tests whether direct analog control offers a runtime advantage over a discrete compiled implementation of the same target gate. Taken together, these methods define a comparison protocol that is not centered on a single optimizer, but on a set of scientifically distinct questions concerning accuracy, robustness, and hardware efficiency.

\subsection{Metrics}
The performance of each control method is evaluated through a set of complementary metrics designed to probe different aspects of the control problem. At the most basic level, the implemented gate is assessed through its fidelity with respect to the target unitary. For a final physical propagator \(U(T)\), projected onto the computational subspace, the gate fidelity is computed as
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathrm{gate}}
=
\frac{1}{16}
\left|
\mathrm{Tr}\!\left(
U_{\mathrm{proj}}^{\dagger}(T)\,U_{\mathrm{target}}
\right)
\right|^2,
\label{eq:validation_gate_fidelity}
\end{equation}
where \(U_{\mathrm{target}}=N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)\). This quantity is reported together with the total UFO cost, which combines fidelity, leakage, boundary penalties, and runtime into a single scalar objective. Since the framework is explicitly multilevel, nominal performance is not characterized by fidelity alone. For every optimized control plan, the simulation also returns the TSWT-based leakage estimate \(L_{\mathrm{tot}}\), its decomposition into boundary and integral contributions, the boundary penalty term, and the final runtime in nanoseconds. These quantities allow one to distinguish between a control that is merely accurate on the logical subspace and one that is also physically well behaved in the full truncated Hilbert space. In addition to nominal performance, robustness is evaluated statistically under sampled noisy control realizations. Given a control plan \(\tilde{\bm{c}}\) and a Gaussian noise level \(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}\), the key robustness diagnostics are the average fidelity
\begin{equation}
\overline{F}
=
\mathbb{E}_{\delta\tilde{\bm{c}}}
\Bigl[
F\bigl(U(\tilde{\bm{c}}+\delta\tilde{\bm{c}})\bigr)
\Bigr],
\label{eq:validation_average_fidelity}
\end{equation}
the corresponding average gate fidelity, and the fidelity variance
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(F)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\delta\tilde{\bm{c}}}
\left[
\bigl(F-\overline{F}\bigr)^2
\right].
\label{eq:validation_fidelity_variance}
\end{equation}
These metrics are essential because they distinguish mean robustness from shot-to-shot stability: a control scheme with high average fidelity but large variance remains experimentally fragile. Finally, for the runtime study, the relevant metric is the total control duration required to realize a target gate, compared against the optimal discrete gate-synthesis baseline. The resulting evaluation protocol is therefore explicitly multidimensional: it measures logical accuracy, leakage suppression, robustness under stochastic perturbations, and hardware efficiency rather than relying on a single scalar score.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
All experiments follow a common procedural logic. For a given target gate \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)\), a control method is first trained or optimized under its own learning rule until either a prescribed iteration budget is exhausted or a termination criterion on the UFO cost is satisfied. The resulting best control plan is then stored and re-evaluated in a deterministic nominal setting in order to extract its final cost, fidelity, leakage, and runtime under identical physical assumptions. For robustness studies, the same saved control plan is subsequently subjected to repeated noisy rollouts, in which Gaussian perturbations are independently sampled and injected into the control channels and into the anharmonicity-related parameter of the Hamiltonian. This Monte Carlo evaluation yields the empirical estimates entering Eqs.~\eqref{eq:validation_average_fidelity} and \eqref{eq:validation_fidelity_variance}. In the runtime study, by contrast, the focus is not on statistical robustness but on how the optimized gate duration varies across the target family, especially along the subfamily \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\), where analog control can be meaningfully compared with optimal gate synthesis. The same timestep, control bounds, bandwidth assumptions, and physical truncation are retained throughout, so that differences between methods can be attributed to the optimization strategy rather than to inconsistencies in the simulation model. This common evaluation protocol is particularly important for the interpretation of the results: it ensures that the subsequent comparisons are methodologically fair, while also making the distinction between nominal accuracy, noisy robustness, and runtime efficiency completely explicit.



\section{Parameter-choice study}
\label{sec:parameter_choice_study}
\subsection{Aim and Structure}
\label{subsec:parameter_choice_aim_and_structure}
The empirical performance of the present control framework depends not only on the underlying physical model and on the definition of the UFO objective, but also on a number of methodological choices that must be fixed before a meaningful comparison can be carried out. This is especially true in the reinforcement-learning setting considered in this thesis, where optimization quality is affected simultaneously by the training configuration, by the expressive capacity of the policy and value networks, and by the scalarization adopted in the control cost. For this reason, parameter selection should not be treated as a minor implementation detail. On the contrary, it constitutes an explicit part of the experimental methodology, since different choices may alter convergence stability, final gate quality, robustness under noise, and even the apparent relative performance of the compared optimizers.

A further reason for discussing parameter choice carefully is that the reference framework of Niu \emph{et al.} fixes several ingredients rather explicitly. 
% In particolare, the original study adopts a continuous-action TRPO agent with two fully connected neural networks of hidden sizes \(64\), \(32\), and \(32\), updates the policy after batches of \(20000\) episodes, injects Gaussian amplitude fluctuations with a standard deviation of \(1\,\mathrm{MHz}\) during stochastic training, and uses the same UFO weight configuration \(\chi=\beta=10\), \(\mu=0.2\), \(\kappa=0.1\) across the target gates considered. The same paper also evaluates robustness on the representative gate \(N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2)\), which therefore provides a natural reference target for model-selection purposes in the present reproduction. 
These design choices are important because they define the baseline notion of a \emph{fair comparison} with the original framework: if the thesis changes some of them, this should be done deliberately and justified explicitly, rather than implicitly through undocumented implementation drift.
% :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0} :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2} :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
At the same time, the goal of this section is not to claim a globally exhaustive hyperparameter optimization over the full design space. Such a program would be computationally prohibitive and would add little scientific value to the main questions of the thesis. The objective is instead more focused: to identify a parameter regime that is stable, reproducible, physically sensible, and sufficiently well aligned with the reference paper to support a credible reproduction study. 
% In other words, the purpose of the parameter-choice stage is to reduce arbitrariness, not to optimize every numerical setting in isolation. 
% This distinction is methodologically important. 
% A thesis-level validation study should not depend on a hidden collection of ad hoc choices tuned separately for each experiment; it should rely on a small and transparent set of decisions that are fixed once and then used consistently throughout the empirical chapter.

For clarity, the parameter-selection analysis is therefore decomposed into three separate studies. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item The first concerns both \emph{training hyperparameters} and \emph{simulation settings}, such as the timestep, maximum horizon, trust-region radius, exploration scale, batch size, and termination criterion. These parameters primarily affect optimization stability, sample efficiency, and computational cost. 
    \item The second concerns the \emph{neural-network architecture} used for the policy and value functions. This is treated separately because the expressive capacity of the actor--critic representation is not simply another training detail: it may materially influence the quality of the learned control policy and therefore must be distinguished from purely algorithmic settings. 
    \item The third concerns the \emph{UFO cost-function weights}, which play an even more special role. Unlike ordinary optimizer hyperparameters, these coefficients are part of the physical definition of the control objective itself, since they determine how gate infidelity, leakage, boundary regularity, and runtime are balanced against one another. For this reason, architecture choice and cost-function choice should not be conflated with the tuning of TRPO-specific parameters.
\end{itemize}

This separation is also useful from the viewpoint of interpretation. If network size and UFO weights were tuned simultaneously together with training hyperparameters, any later performance difference would become harder to attribute: an observed improvement could arise from a better optimizer configuration, from increased representational capacity, or from a different physical scalarization of the control problem. By isolating the three studies, the discussion becomes much cleaner. The reader can first understand which choices are made for numerical stability, then which are made for representational adequacy, and finally which affect the physical tradeoff structure encoded in the reward. This is particularly important in the present thesis, because one of its goals is not only to reproduce the original framework, but also to clarify which parts of its performance are tied to the learning algorithm and which depend more directly on the structure of the cost function.

Finally, the section closes by fixing one final parameter configuration for the remainder of the chapter. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, all later experiments will be performed under that single configuration, so that the subsequent comparisons are not biased by experiment-specific retuning.

Section~\ref{subsec:parameter_selection_tradeoffs} discusses the training hyperparameters and the practical trade-offs that motivate their final selection. The following subsection is devoted to the sweep over policy and value network sizes, with the aim of determining whether the paper architecture is already sufficient for the present implementation or whether a different capacity yields a more stable control regime. After that, a dedicated sensitivity study of the UFO coefficients is introduced, in order to separate the role of the physical objective from that of the optimizer itself.

\subsection{Simulation and training parameters choice}
\label{subsec:simulation_and_training_parameters_choice}
\paragraph{Simulation parameters.}
\label{subsec:simulation_parameters}

Before discussing the optimizer-specific settings, it is useful to isolate those numerical choices that belong more directly to the simulation environment itself. These parameters do not define the physical control objective in the same sense as the UFO weights, but neither are they mere training details: they determine the temporal resolution of the control problem, the effective size of the admissible trajectory space, and the computational burden of each rollout. For this reason, they should be discussed separately from the strictly algorithmic hyperparameters of TRPO. As discussed in subsection \ref{subsec:why_this_target_is_representative}, the parameter-selection study is anchored to the representative gate
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{rep}} = N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2),
\label{eq:representative_gate_simulation_study}
\end{equation}
which provides a nontrivial and paper-aligned benchmark for model-selection purposes \cite{Niu2019}. In this way, the simulation-side choices are fixed on a task that is already central to the empirical narrative of the thesis, rather than on an auxiliary or artificially simplified control problem.

Among the simulation parameters, the most consequential are the timestep \(\Delta t\), the maximum admissible gate duration \(T_{\max}\), and the stochastic perturbation scale used during noisy training. These quantities affect the control problem at a more structural level than the remaining optimizer settings. The timestep controls the temporal granularity with which the pulse can be shaped, and therefore the expressive power of the sequential decision process. A smaller \(\Delta t\) gives access to a richer control mesh and can in principle improve the ability of the controller to coordinate fidelity, leakage suppression, and runtime. However, it also increases the episode length, enlarges the effective action horizon, and makes each multilevel rollout more expensive. Likewise, increasing \(T_{\max}\) enlarges the reachable set of pulse trajectories, but it also expands the search space and weakens the hardware-efficiency motivation of analog control, since excessively long solutions are less relevant from the perspective of superconducting-qubit implementation.

In the present implementation, these trade-offs led to the default simulation choice
\begin{equation}
\Delta t = 2\,\mathrm{ns},
\qquad
T_{\max}=180\,\mathrm{ns},
\label{eq:default_simulation_discretization}
\end{equation}
which represents a practical compromise between temporal flexibility and computational tractability. This discretization is fine enough to support nontrivial pulse shaping in the multilevel gmon model, while keeping the horizon sufficiently short for repeated policy rollouts and robustness evaluations to remain feasible. In this sense, the choice in Eq.~\eqref{eq:default_simulation_discretization} should not be interpreted as numerically arbitrary, but as the point at which improved control resolution ceases to justify the additional simulation cost within the present thesis workflow.

A related but conceptually distinct ingredient is the stochastic perturbation model used during noisy training. In continuity with the original UFO framework, the present implementation injects Gaussian control perturbations with standard deviation
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{train}} = 1\,\mathrm{MHz}
\label{eq:default_simulation_training_noise}
\end{equation}
into the relevant control channels during stochastic training \cite{Niu2019}. This value is retained not only to preserve comparability with the reference framework, but also because it defines the default robust-training regime used in the rest of the empirical chapter. The broader robustness analysis will later evaluate each controller across a wider interval of noise strengths, but the value in Eq.~\eqref{eq:default_simulation_training_noise} fixes the perturbation scale that is built directly into the training environment.

Taken together, the choices in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:default_simulation_discretization} and \eqref{eq:default_simulation_training_noise} define the default simulation environment adopted throughout the thesis. They determine the temporal and stochastic structure within which the controller operates, and therefore set the stage on which the optimizer-specific hyperparameters can be meaningfully discussed.

\paragraph{Training hyperparameters}
\label{subsec:training_hyperparameters}

\paragraph{Training hyperparameters}
\label{subsec:training_hyperparameters}

Once the simulation environment has been fixed, the next layer of parameter selection concerns the hyperparameters of the reinforcement-learning algorithm itself. These do not change the physical control problem, but they strongly affect optimization stability, exploration behavior, statistical efficiency, and wall-time cost. For this reason, they should be distinguished from the simulation-side choices discussed above, even though the two levels are obviously coupled in practice.

Within this optimizer-side configuration, the most relevant parameters are the trust-region radius (\(\delta_{\mathrm{KL}}\)), the discount factor (\(\gamma_{\mathrm{RL}}\)), the GAE parameter (\(\lambda\)), the initial exploration scale (\(\log \sigma_0\)), the batch size measured in episodes per policy update, the number of training iterations, and the terminal-cost threshold used for early stopping. Their roles are standard in the TRPO literature. In particular, the KL bound controls how aggressively the policy is allowed to move at each update, while \(\gamma_{\mathrm{RL}}\) and \(\lambda\) regulate temporal credit assignment and the bias--variance trade-off in the advantage estimator; in continuous-control settings, these choices are closely tied to the stability of policy-gradient updates with neural-network function approximation \cite{Schulman2015,Schulman2016GAE}. For broader background on discounted returns and temporal-difference methods, see also \cite{SuttonBarto2018}.

Among these parameters, the clearest trade-off is the one between exploration and stability. If \(\log \sigma_{0}\) is chosen too large, early training becomes excessively noisy: the policy explores too broadly, the squashed actions are frequently pushed toward saturation, and the resulting control trajectories become unstable and difficult to refine. If it is chosen too small, however, the controller explores too weakly and may fail to discover useful pulse patterns within a reasonable number of iterations. A similar balance appears in the trust-region radius \(\delta_{\mathrm{KL}}\): larger values permit faster progress in principle, but also increase the risk of unstable policy jumps, whereas very small values make learning overly conservative. In the present study, these considerations lead to the nominal optimizer-side choices
\begin{equation}
\log \sigma_0 = -1.0,
\qquad
\delta_{\mathrm{KL}} = 0.01,
\label{eq:default_training_exploration_trustregion}
\end{equation}
together with the standard TRPO-side settings
\begin{equation}
\gamma_{\mathrm{RL}}=0.99,
\qquad
\lambda=0.97,
\qquad
\texttt{cg\_iters}=10,
\qquad
\texttt{damping}=0.1.
\label{eq:default_training_auxiliary_parameters}
\end{equation}
These values should not be interpreted as theoretically unique or universally optimal. Rather, they define an empirically stable operating regime, consistent with standard TRPO/GAE practice and with the reinforcement-learning formulation adopted in the original UFO framework \cite{Schulman2015,Schulman2016GAE,Niu2019}.

A further methodological compromise concerns the relation between stopping threshold, batch size, and total iteration budget. A loose termination criterion reduces training time, but risks accepting controls that are only superficially satisfactory. A stricter threshold is scientifically preferable, since it forces the optimizer to search for genuinely low-cost solutions, but it is also harder to satisfy reliably within a finite computational budget. In the present implementation, the default early-stopping threshold is set to
\begin{equation}
C_{\mathrm{term}}=0.2,
\label{eq:default_training_termination_cost}
\end{equation}
which is intentionally demanding and therefore avoids premature convergence toward mediocre solutions. While more exploratory sweeps, such as those reported in Sections~\ref{subsec:nn_size_sweep} and \ref{subsec:ufo_weight_sweep}, were conducted with the looser threshold \(C_{\mathrm{term}}=0.4\), the main comparison runs retained the stricter value. Likewise, the larger-batch setting used in the main experiments was chosen to reduce gradient noise and improve the stability of on-policy updates, while lighter preliminary sweeps employed smaller batches for computational convenience.

The resulting default optimizer-side configuration can therefore be summarized as
\begin{equation}
\log \sigma_0 = -1.0,\quad
\delta_{\mathrm{KL}} = 0.01,\quad
C_{\mathrm{term}}=0.20,
\label{eq:default_training_configuration_summary_revised}
\end{equation}
with \(\gamma_{\mathrm{RL}}=0.99\), \(\lambda=0.97\), \(10\) conjugate-gradient iterations, damping \(0.1\), and the batch-size choices specified above. This configuration is not claimed to be uniquely optimal, but it provides a coherent and transparent training regime under which later comparisons can be interpreted without ambiguity.

\subsection{Neural-network size sweep for policy and value functions}
\label{subsec:nn_size_sweep}

In the original UFO framework, both the policy and value functions are represented by fully connected neural networks with three hidden layers of sizes \(64\), \(32\), and \(32\) \cite{Niu2019}. Since the present implementation also relies on a continuous-action actor--critic TRPO pipeline, the architecture of these networks is not a negligible detail. The hidden-layer layout directly affects the representational capacity of the policy, the stability of the value-function approximation, the efficiency of exploration in the continuous control space, and ultimately the quality of the learned gate-control strategy. For this reason, the choice of network size should be treated separately from the tuning of training hyperparameters and from the selection of the UFO cost weights.

The purpose of the present subsection is therefore to assess whether modest changes in network width and depth materially affect performance in the current implementation. Importantly, this study is not intended as an exhaustive architecture search over a large design space. Rather, it is a restricted sweep aimed at answering a narrower question: within a family of architectures of comparable scale, does increasing width or depth improve the learned balance between control quality, leakage suppression, and robustness? This question is especially relevant because the reference paper motivates the use of neural networks as compact representations of non-local regularities in successful control trajectories \cite{Niu2019}. If that interpretation is correct, then architecture should matter, but not necessarily in a monotonic way: larger networks may provide more expressive power, yet also introduce harder optimization and greater seed sensitivity.

The sweep was performed on the same representative target gate used for the training-parameter study,
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{rep}} = N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2),
\label{eq:nn_sweep_representative_target}
\end{equation}
and under a fixed reduced-budget training protocol designed for model selection.
More precisely, all runs used noise-optimized TRPO, termination threshold \(C_{\mathrm{term}}=0.4\), and the nominal UFO weights
\begin{equation}
(\chi,\beta,\mu,\kappa)=(10,10,0.2,0.1).
\label{eq:nn_sweep_default_weights}
\end{equation}
Each architecture was trained for \(20\) TRPO iterations with \(5000\) episodes per batch and evaluated over \(3\) random seeds.\footnote{The remaining parameters were fixed as described in subsection~\ref{subsec:simulation_and_training_parameters_choice}.}
The tested hidden-layer layouts were
\begin{equation}
(64,32,32),\qquad
(64,64,64),\qquad
(64,64,64,32),\qquad
(128,64,32),\qquad
(128,64,32,32).
\label{eq:tested_hidden_sizes}
\end{equation}

To compare the candidate networks, several criteria were considered simultaneously. The primary selection metric was the best evaluation cost reached during training, since this is the quantity most directly aligned with the UFO objective. However, this scalar alone is not sufficient for a meaningful choice. A network could achieve a favorable total cost by trading leakage against fidelity or by settling into an unnecessarily long runtime. For this reason, the architecture comparison also tracked best evaluation fidelity, best evaluation leakage, effective evaluation runtime, and robustness under noisy deployment through the average fidelity at \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\). In this way, the architecture was not chosen solely by one scalar score, but by its overall position in the multi-objective trade-off relevant to the control problem.

The results of the completed architecture sweep are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:architecture_sweep_results}. The first important observation is that the architecture \(64\text{-}64\text{-}64\) still achieves the best overall mean evaluation cost, together with the strongest mean robustness at \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\). In this restricted-budget setting, it therefore remains the strongest architecture in terms of the aggregated objective. However, the inclusion of the previously missing paper architecture \(64\text{-}32\text{-}32\) changes the interpretation in a nontrivial way. Its mean evaluation cost is only marginally worse than that of \(64\text{-}64\text{-}64\), while it achieves a slightly higher mean nominal fidelity and a lower mean leakage. In other words, the original paper layout is not dominated by a large margin; rather, it lies very close to the best-performing region of the sweep.

This updated picture suggests a more nuanced conclusion than the preliminary one. The architecture \(64\text{-}64\text{-}64\) remains the best choice if the objective is to optimize the aggregate reduced-budget performance as aggressively as possible. Nevertheless, the gap with \(64\text{-}32\text{-}32\) is small enough that the latter should now be regarded as a genuinely competitive alternative rather than as a clearly inferior baseline. This point matters methodologically, because \(64\text{-}32\text{-}32\) is the exact architecture reported in the original paper \cite{Niu2019}; retaining it in later experiments is therefore still fully defensible when faithfulness to the reference framework is prioritized over marginal gains in the reduced-budget sweep.

The deeper alternatives do not improve this picture. The architecture \(64\text{-}64\text{-}64\text{-}32\) performs worse than both leading candidates in all principal aggregated metrics, with a higher mean evaluation cost, a lower mean fidelity, and a visibly weaker robustness profile. The architecture \(128\text{-}64\text{-}32\) remains close in mean cost, but does not offer a compensating advantage in fidelity, leakage, or robustness. The deepest tested model, \(128\text{-}64\text{-}32\text{-}32\), is particularly instructive: it achieves the \emph{lowest mean leakage} of the sweep, but also the \emph{worst} mean evaluation cost, the lowest mean robustness, and the weakest mean nominal fidelity. This indicates once again that minimizing leakage in isolation is not an adequate architecture-selection principle. A larger or deeper network may learn a more conservative control strategy with somewhat cleaner multilevel confinement, yet still fail to provide the best overall balance among the terms entering the UFO objective.

\subsection{Sensitivity study of the UFO cost-function weights}

The choice of the UFO coefficients deserves a separate study because, unlike ordinary optimizer hyperparameters, these quantities are part of the physical definition of the control problem itself. As mentioned in \ref{subsec:ufo_cost_function}, the total objective is written as
\begin{equation}
    C(\chi,\beta,\mu,\kappa)
    =
\chi\bigl[1-F(U(T))\bigr]
+
\beta L_{\mathrm{tot}}
+
\mu P_{\mathrm{bdry}}
+
\kappa T,
\label{eq:ufo_cost_recalled_weight_sweep}
\end{equation}
where \(\chi\) controls the weight of gate infidelity, \(\beta\) penalizes accumulated leakage, \(\mu\) enforces boundary regularity, and \(\kappa\) penalizes runtime \cite{Niu2019}. The reference paper reports that these coefficients were selected by grid search and then fixed uniformly across the target gates considered, with the final choice
\begin{equation}
    \chi=\beta=10,
    \qquad
    \mu=0.2,
    \qquad
    \kappa=0.1.
    \label{eq:paper_ufo_weights_recalled}
\end{equation}
For the purposes of the present thesis, this makes the weight study methodologically important in two complementary senses. On the one hand, it allows one to test whether the balance adopted in the paper is also reasonable in the present independent implementation. On the other hand, it clarifies which aspects of the observed performance are due to the optimizer and which are instead induced directly by the physical scalarization of the control objective.


The sweep was performed under the same parameter choices described in \ref{subsec:simulation_and_training_parameters_choice}, and the paper-matched network architecture \(64\text{-}32\text{-}32\). Two random seeds were used for each configuration. The explored coefficient ranges were
\begin{equation}
    \chi \in \{5,10,20\},
    \qquad
\beta = 10,
\qquad
\mu \in \{0.1,0.2,0.4\},
\qquad
\kappa \in \{0.05,0.1,0.2,0.4\},
\label{eq:ufo_weight_sweep_ranges}
\end{equation}
for a total of \(3\times 1\times 3\times 4 = 36\) weight configurations and \(72\) runs once the two seeds are included.

The choice to keep \(\beta\) fixed deserves an explicit comment. Since the goal of the present experiment is to understand how the optimizer reacts to changing the balance between fidelity, boundary regularity, and runtime \emph{relative} to leakage suppression, it is natural to treat \(\beta\) as a reference scale and vary the other coefficients around it. In this sense, the sweep should be interpreted primarily through the ratios \(\chi/\beta\), \(\mu/\beta\), and \(\kappa/\beta\), rather than as a completely unconstrained four-dimensional search. This restriction also keeps the interpretation manageable: if all four coefficients were varied simultaneously, it would become much harder to isolate which part of the scalarization is actually responsible for a given change in behavior and it would increase further the computational cost of the sweep.

A second methodological point is even more important: the \emph{best evaluation cost} is not an informative global selection criterion across this sweep. Indeed, different values of \(\chi\), \(\mu\), and \(\kappa\) define different scalarizations of the same physical control problem, so the numerical value of \(C(\chi,\beta,\mu,\kappa)\) is not directly comparable across configurations. This is not just a formal issue. In the present experiment, all runs terminate at the same evaluation time of \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\), so increasing \(\kappa\) contributes mainly a nearly rigid additive shift to the total cost without necessarily improving the learned control in any physically meaningful sense. For this reason, the search-script primary metric is retained only as an internal bookkeeping quantity, whereas the physically relevant comparison is based instead on nominal fidelity, best evaluation leakage, and robustness average fidelity at \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\).

Under this interpretation, the first clear result concerns the fidelity weight \(\chi\). The average fidelity boxplot in Fig.~\ref{fig:ufo_weight_sweep_robustness} shows that \(\chi=5\) leads to a visibly broader and lower-performing distribution, with several runs collapsing into a poor-average-fidelity regime around \(0.81\)--\(0.83\). By contrast, \(\chi=10\) and \(\chi=20\) both yield substantially tighter and higher average-fidelity distributions, concentrated around \(0.92\)--\(0.95\). This indicates that the low-fidelity-penalty regime underweights the logical-accuracy term too strongly relative to the other penalties. 
% In other words, if \(\chi\) is chosen too small compared with \(\beta\), the optimizer is allowed to settle into controls that may be acceptable in terms of leakage or boundary behavior, yet remain clearly inferior in actual gate quality. Increasing \(\chi\) to the paper scale already removes most of this failure mode, while a further increase to \(\chi=20\) produces only a marginal additional gain.

The behavior of the boundary weight \(\mu\) is more subtle and, in some sense, more interesting. The sweep reveals a clear non-monotonic pattern. The intermediate value
\begin{equation}
    \mu = 0.2
    \label{eq:mu_intermediate_best}
\end{equation}
produces the most favorable median trade-off, with high average fidelity and good nominal fidelity. Reducing the penalty to \(\mu=0.1\) remains viable, but leads on average to slightly larger leakage and a somewhat less favorable average fidelity profile. Increasing it to \(\mu=0.4\), however, makes the distribution strongly bimodal: for some values of \(\chi\) it yields some of the best runs of the entire sweep, while for others it produces a catastrophic degradation in average fidelity. This is clearly visible in the average fidelity boxplot, where \(\mu=0.4\) generates the widest spread and the strongest lower-tail failures. The physical interpretation is that boundary regularization is beneficial only up to a point. A moderate boundary penalty encourages smoother pulse endpoints, but an overly strong one can overconstrain the optimizer and distort the balance with the fidelity term, thereby harming the overall control quality rather than improving it.

This interpretation is reinforced when leakage is examined separately. Figure~\ref{fig:ufo_weight_sweep_leakage} shows that larger \(\mu\) tends, on average, to reduce the leakage proxy. In fact, the lowest-leakage region of the sweep is often associated with \(\mu=0.4\). However, this does \emph{not} translate automatically into the best robust controller. The reason is precisely that leakage is only one component of the total physical objective. In several runs, the stronger boundary penalty helps suppress leakage but simultaneously worsens the broader fidelity--robustness trade-off. 
This is therefore an example of the fact that minimizing one term of the cost more aggressively does not guarantee a better overall control solution.

The runtime coefficient \(\kappa\) has the weakest effect in the present restricted-budget sweep. This is visible both in the average fidelity sensitivity plot and in the leakage sensitivity plot, where the four tested values

\(\kappa\in\{0.05,0.1,0.2,0.4\}\) produce distributions that are almost indistinguishable at the level of median performance. This weak dependence is not surprising once the evaluation protocol is taken into account. Since every run reaches the same best evaluation time of \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\), the runtime term does not act here as a strong discriminating mechanism on the learned stopping time, but mainly as a reweighting of the scalar objective. In other words, under this short and fixed-horizon model-selection regime, \(\kappa\) does not yet have enough room to reshape the control strategy as dramatically as \(\chi\) and \(\mu\) do. A stronger effect of \(\kappa\) is more likely to appear in the later runtime-family experiments, where gate duration is itself a central observable rather than an almost fixed boundary condition of the sweep.

The clearest global summary of the experiment is given by the Pareto-style leakage--average fidelity scatter in Fig.~\ref{fig:ufo_weight_sweep_pareto}. Two clusters stand out. The first corresponds to a favorable region with average fidelity around \(0.94\) and leakage in the low-\(10^{-4}\) to mid-\(10^{-4}\) range. The second corresponds to a clearly worse region with average fidelity around \(0.81\)--\(0.83\), associated mainly with the underweighted-fidelity settings. The color coding by \(\chi\) makes the main trend immediately visible: low \(\chi\) is overrepresented in the poor-average-fidelity cluster, whereas the paper-scale and larger-\(\chi\) settings dominate the Pareto-favorable part of the plane. At the same time, the frontier is not determined by \(\chi\) alone. The best region is occupied jointly by configurations near
\begin{equation}
(\chi,\mu) \approx (10,0.2)
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
(\chi,\mu) \approx (20,0.4),
\label{eq:ufo_weight_frontier_region}
\end{equation}
with only weak dependence on \(\kappa\) within the explored range.

This last observation is crucial for the final choice. The numerically best average fidelity is attained by the family of configurations centered around \((\chi,\beta,\mu)=(20,10,0.4)\), but the gain over the paper choice
\((10,10,0.2)\) is extremely small. In the present sweep, the paper configuration remains essentially on the same Pareto-favorable frontier, with very similar average fidelity and leakage, while being directly aligned with the scalarization reported in the original study \cite{Niu2019}. Since the improvement offered by the larger-\(\chi\), larger-\(\mu\) corner is marginal and the present sweep is based on only two seeds and a very small training budget, there is no strong methodological reason to abandon the paper weights for the main experiments. On the contrary, keeping the reference choice
\begin{equation}
(\chi,\beta,\mu,\kappa)=(10,10,0.2,0.1)
\label{eq:selected_ufo_weights_final}
\end{equation}
is preferable for two reasons: it remains empirically near-optimal in the present implementation, and it preserves the cleanest possible fair-comparison protocol with the original UFO framework.

The main conclusion of this subsection is therefore twofold. First, the cost weights matter, and they matter in a physically interpretable way: too small a fidelity weight degrades average fidelity substantially, while an excessively strong boundary penalty can reduce leakage at the price of destabilizing the broader control trade-off. Second, the paper scalarization is not only historically motivated, but also empirically reasonable in the present independent implementation. For this reason, the remainder of the empirical chapter will retain the reference UFO coefficients in Eq.~\eqref{eq:selected_ufo_weights_final}, unless explicitly stated otherwise.

\subsection{Justification for the choice of target}
\label{subsec:why_this_target_is_representative}

\begin{itemize}
    \item First, it is a genuinely nontrivial target: it is neither close to the identity nor reducible to a particularly simple limiting case, and therefore provides a meaningful test of the optimizer's ability to coordinate entangling dynamics, local control, and leakage suppression within the full multilevel model.
    \item Second, it is directly aligned with the original benchmark logic of the reference paper, which uses the same gate family as the main testbed for robustness and runtime analyses \cite{Niu2019}. For this reason, selecting a target of the form \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\) allows the present study to remain as close as possible to the original physical and algorithmic setting while still isolating one concrete case for detailed discussion.
    \item Third, fixing a single representative gate provides a clean comparison between optimization strategies before moving to broader parameter sweeps: by holding the target fixed, differences in final fidelity, leakage, runtime, and training behavior can be attributed more directly to the optimizer itself rather than to variations in target-gate difficulty across the family.
\end{itemize}
In this sense, Eq.~\eqref{eq:representative_target_gate} serves as the natural entry point for the empirical analysis, since it is sufficiently rich to be physically informative while remaining simple enough to support a transparent method-by-method comparison.

The dependence on the gate duration is particularly instructive. Very short horizons, such as \(40\,\mathrm{ns}\) and \(50\,\mathrm{ns}\), perform poorly: the nominal fidelities drop to \(0.7255\) and \(0.8818\), while the corresponding boundary penalties remain very large, respectively \(0.399\) and \(0.357\). This means that the main failure mode of the ultra-short solutions is \emph{not} an isolated explosion of leakage alone, but the inability to satisfy simultaneously the accuracy and boundary requirements of the UFO objective under a strongly time-compressed pulse. This behavior is fully consistent with the speed--leakage and speed--smoothness tension discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:speed_leakage_tradeoff}: in weakly anharmonic superconducting systems, shortening the pulse too aggressively broadens the effective control spectrum and makes it more difficult to maintain both clean logical evolution and well-behaved pulse boundaries \cite{Motzoi2009,Koch2022}. The sharp transition from \(60\,\mathrm{ns}\) to \(70\,\mathrm{ns}\) is especially revealing in this respect. Over this interval, the fidelity increases from \(0.982\) to \(0.997\), the boundary term collapses from \(9.75\times10^{-2}\) to \(4.28\times10^{-3}\), and the total nominal cost drops from \(0.381\) to \(0.158\), whereas the leakage decreases only moderately. This indicates that, near the optimum, the decisive improvement is not merely less, but the fact that the pulse becomes sufficiently long to realize the target unitary while also respecting the boundary structure required by the UFO cost.

From the nominal-performance viewpoint, this result is already strong in absolute terms. A deterministic fidelity above \(99.4\%\) on the full multilevel model confirms that the policy-gradient formulation is able to learn a nontrivial high-quality analog control for the representative target. Moreover, the learned runtime of \(116\,\mathrm{ns}\) remains substantially below the \(215\,\mathrm{ns}\) synthesized-gate reference quoted in the original paper for the optimal circuit decomposition of the same target family \cite{Niu2019}. In other words, even before introducing noise-aware training, the nominal TRPO controller already supports the more general claim that direct analog control can exploit hardware-native structure to outperform standard compiled implementations in runtime. The gain is, however, more moderate than in the strongest analog-control cases emphasized by the paper: for the present target, the reduction is approximately
\begin{equation}
\frac{215\,\mathrm{ns}}{116\,\mathrm{ns}} \approx 1.85,
\label{eq:nominal_trpo_vs_synthesis_factor}
\end{equation}
which is significant, but far from the order-of-magnitude speedups later reported for the noise-optimized runtime sweeps across the broader gate family \cite{Niu2019}.

At the same time, when this nominal TRPO solution is compared with the Adam baseline of the previous subsection, an instructive picture emerges. More explicitly, TRPO is worse than Adam on this single deterministic target both in total cost (\(0.2982\) vs. \(0.1576\)) and in gate duration (\(116\,\mathrm{ns}\) vs. \(70\,\mathrm{ns}\)), while its leakage is larger by roughly a factor of

This comparison is relevant, because it prevents an over-interpretation of the RL method. On the representative clean target, nominal TRPO does \emph{not} dominate a strong direct pulse optimizer. Instead, the deterministic single-target benchmark suggests that, when the model is known accurately and the objective is smooth and fully differentiable, direct gradient-based optimization remains highly competitive, and in the present case superior, in the purely nominal regime. This is broadly consistent with the standard optimal-control literature, where direct gradient methods such as GRAPE are known to be particularly effective on fixed-target closed-system pulse-design problems \cite{Khaneja2005,Koch2022}.

This observation is not a weakness of the RL framework; rather, it clarifies what the RL framework should be expected to contribute. The original paper does not primarily claim that TRPO is universally better than direct optimization on every nominal benchmark. Its stronger claim is instead that a policy-based method trained in the appropriate stochastic environment can improve \emph{robustness} and, in some hardware-aligned families, \emph{runtime efficiency} relative to simpler baselines \cite{Niu2019}. The present nominal-clean result is therefore valuable precisely because it isolates what TRPO does \emph{without} the noise-aware component: it learns a high-fidelity analog gate and a nontrivial variable-time control strategy, but it does not yet exhibit an intrinsic nominal advantage over a well-tuned direct optimizer on this representative deterministic target. This makes the next subsection conceptually much stronger, because any improvement observed there can be attributed more specifically to stochastic training rather than to the mere use of reinforcement learning.

A second useful insight concerns the qualitative role of the UFO scalarization. The nominal TRPO controller reaches a reasonable deterministic fidelity, but it does so at a larger runtime and with substantially more leakage than the best Adam solution. This suggests that, in the clean setting, the policy has found a more conservative but less sharply optimized compromise in the multi-objective landscape. Put differently, the RL controller appears able to discover acceptable controls within the admissible region of the UFO cost, but in the absence of explicit noise pressure it has less incentive to carve out the particularly efficient low-leakage solution that a direct horizon-conditioned optimizer can reach on the same target. This interpretation is fully consistent with the control-theoretic discussion in Sections~\ref{subsec:classical_qoc_methods}, \ref{subsec:why_rl_is_relevant}, and \ref{subsec:reward_design_bridge_physics_rl}: policy optimization is especially attractive when sequential structure, uncertainty, and robustness matter, but it need not be the most aggressive optimizer of a single deterministic pulse-shaping problem.

Finally, the nominal TRPO experiment also motivates a careful reading of training curves. Because the controller is evaluated after every policy update and the code stores both the current evaluation cost and the time \(t_{\min}\) at which the minimum rollout cost is reached, the most informative training visualization is not simply fidelity versus iteration. A better representation is one that jointly tracks the evolution of evaluation cost, fidelity, leakage, and learned stopping time. In the present case, such a plot would show that the policy quickly enters a high-fidelity regime but continues to trade off leakage and runtime within the admissible \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\) window, eventually settling on a best deterministic solution around the \(116\,\mathrm{ns}\) scale. This makes the nominal TRPO subsection a natural bridge between the fixed-horizon analysis of the Adam baseline and the explicitly robustness-oriented analysis of the next subsection.

\section{Robustness under stochastic control noise}
\label{sec:robustness_under_stochastic_control_noise}
\subsection{Noise model}
\label{subsec:noise_model}
To assess robustness in a physically meaningful and methodologically consistent way, all controllers considered in this section are evaluated under the same stochastic control-error model, following the general logic of the original UFO framework \cite{Niu2019}. At the discrete time-step level, the nominal control trajectory is not implemented exactly, but is perturbed by additive Gaussian amplitude fluctuations. In the notation of the control vector introduced in Eq.~\eqref{eq:validation_control_vector}, this means that the realized control at time step \(t_k\) takes the schematic form
\begin{equation}
\bm{u}^{(\mathrm{noisy})}(t_k)
=
\bm{u}(t_k)+\bm{\xi}(t_k),
\label{eq:robustness_noisy_control_vector}
\end{equation}
where the noise term \(\bm{\xi}(t_k)\) is sampled independently at each time step from a zero-mean Gaussian distribution. In line with both the original paper and the present implementation, these fluctuations are applied not only to the directly actuated control amplitudes, namely the coupling pulse \(g(t)\), the detuning amplitudes \(\delta_j(t)\), and the microwave amplitudes \(f_j(t)\), but also to the anharmonicity-related parameter \(\eta\), which enters the Hamiltonian as part of the multilevel device model \cite{Niu2019}. This point is important: the noise model is not restricted to imperfect pulse delivery alone, but also probes sensitivity to uncertainty in a physically relevant Hamiltonian parameter.

At the Hamiltonian level, each noisy rollout is therefore generated by replacing the nominal control-dependent evolution with a perturbed one-step propagator,
\begin{equation}
U_{k+1}
=
\exp\!\left[
-i\Delta t\,
\bigl(
\hat{H}(t_k)+\delta \hat{H}(t_k)
\bigr)
\right]
U_k,
\label{eq:robustness_noisy_propagator}
\end{equation}


where \(\delta \hat{H}(t_k)\) collects the Gaussian perturbations induced by the noisy realization of the control parameters and of \(\eta\). The overall noise strength is parametrized by a standard deviation \(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}\), which is varied systematically in the robustness study below. In this way, robustness is not inferred from a single perturbed trajectory, but from a family of stochastic control channels of increasing severity.

A further methodological distinction must be made between \emph{training noise} and \emph{evaluation noise}. For the Adam baseline and the nominal TRPO controller, optimization is performed in the deterministic environment, and robustness is assessed only \emph{a posteriori} through noisy Monte Carlo rollouts. By contrast, for the noise-optimized TRPO agent, the same Gaussian perturbation model is already activated during training, so that the policy is learned in a stochastic environment rather than in a nominal one. This distinction is central for the interpretation of the results: if the noise-optimized TRPO controller later exhibits both higher average fidelity and lower fidelity variance under noisy evaluation, then the improvement cannot be attributed to reinforcement learning in general, but specifically to the fact that robustness has been learned from exposure to the stochastic environment itself \cite{Niu2019}.

The noisy performance of a given controller is then characterized statistically by repeated sampling. For each value of \(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}\), a fixed optimized control plan is executed over many independent noisy realizations, and the resulting empirical distribution of final fidelities is used to estimate both the average fidelity and the fidelity variance. In other words, the robustness metrics reported in the next paragraphs are not single-shot quantities, but Monte Carlo estimates over an ensemble of noisy trajectories,
\begin{equation}
\overline{F}(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}})
=
\mathbb{E}_{\delta\bm{u}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}})}
\left[
F\bigl(U(\bm{u}+\delta\bm{u})\bigr)
\right],
\label{eq:robustness_average_fidelity_section}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\overline{F}(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}})
=
\mathbb{E}_{\delta\bm{u},\,\delta\eta}
\left[
F\bigl(U(\bm{u}+\delta\bm{u};\,\eta+\delta\eta)\bigr)
\right],
\label{eq:robustness_average_fidelity_section_eta}
\end{equation}

and
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(F)(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}})
=
\mathbb{E}_{\delta\bm{u}}
\left[
\bigl(F-\overline{F}\bigr)^2
\right].
\label{eq:robustness_fidelity_variance_section}
\end{equation}
This evaluation protocol is particularly well suited to the present thesis, because it directly probes the central claim of the UFO framework: a useful control strategy should not only achieve high nominal fidelity, but should remain accurate and stable under realistic stochastic perturbations of the multilevel superconducting-qubit dynamics.

\subsection{Fidelity variance versus noise strength}
\label{subsec:fidelity_variance_vs_noise}

A complementary perspective on robustness is provided by Fig.~\ref{fig:fidelity_variance_vs_noise}, which reports the fidelity variance as a function of the noise strength. While the average-fidelity curve quantifies the mean degradation induced by stochastic control errors, the variance probes a different and equally important aspect, namely the \emph{shot-to-shot stability} of the control strategy. A controller may retain a relatively good average fidelity and yet remain operationally fragile if its performance fluctuates strongly across different noisy realizations. In this sense, the fidelity variance measures the sensitivity of the optimized control to the Gaussian perturbations introduced in Sec.~\ref{subsec:noise_model}, and therefore provides a natural statistical indicator of robustness under repeated experimental execution \cite{Niu2019}. This interpretation is fully consistent with the evaluation protocol adopted here, where the variance is estimated from repeated Monte Carlo realizations of the same optimized control plan at fixed \(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}\).

The first qualitative feature of the figure is that the fidelity variance increases for all three methods as the noise strength grows. This is expected: as the Gaussian perturbations become larger, the distribution of implemented noisy channels broadens, and different realizations of the same nominal pulse generate increasingly dispersed final fidelities. However, the three methods do not broaden at the same rate. The noise-optimized TRPO controller consistently exhibits the lowest variance across the explored range, the Adam baseline occupies an intermediate position, and the nominal TRPO controller displays the largest fluctuations, with a visibly steeper growth in the moderate-to-high noise regime. The ordering is therefore fully consistent with the one observed in the average-fidelity plot: the controller trained in the stochastic environment is not only the one with the best mean noisy performance, but also the one whose performance is least dispersed across repeated noisy realizations.

This result is particularly important for the interpretation of the framework. The comparison between the two TRPO agents is especially clean, since the nominal and noise-optimized controllers share the same policy-gradient machinery, the same physical model, and the same UFO objective, and differ primarily in the environment used during training. The lower variance of the noise-optimized policy therefore cannot be attributed to reinforcement learning in general, but must be understood as a consequence of stochastic training itself. In other words, exposure to noisy dynamics during optimization does not simply improve the mean fidelity under perturbations; it also produces a control policy whose final performance is statistically more stable from one noisy realization to another. This is precisely the type of effect one would expect if the policy had internalized control strategies that are intrinsically less sensitive to random actuation errors.

\subsection{Interpretation}
\label{subsec:robustness_interpretation}

% \textbf{TODO:} Da valutare se inserire l'altro grafico

Taken together, the average-fidelity and fidelity-variance curves provide the clearest empirical validation of the central robustness claim of the present study. The key question is not whether all controllers degrade under stochastic control perturbations, which is unavoidable, but whether training in a noisy environment produces a control strategy that degrades \emph{more gracefully} than one trained only in the nominal setting. From this viewpoint, the most informative comparison is the one between the two TRPO agents. Since the nominal and noise-optimized controllers share the same policy architecture, the same physical model, and the same UFO objective, their different behavior under noisy evaluation must be attributed primarily to the environment used during training. The fact that the noise-optimized policy achieves both higher average fidelity and lower fidelity variance across the explored range of \(\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}\) therefore shows that robustness is not a generic byproduct of reinforcement learning alone, but a property that is effectively learned through stochastic training \cite{Niu2019}.

This point is especially important in light of the nominal single-target comparison discussed earlier. In the deterministic setting, TRPO was shown to be competitive but not superior to the Adam baseline on the representative gate \(N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2)\). In that regime, the use of reinforcement learning by itself did not guarantee the best pulse. The robustness analysis changes this picture in a qualitatively meaningful way. Once performance is evaluated over an ensemble of noisy realizations rather than on a single nominal rollout, the controller trained in the stochastic environment becomes the strongest overall solution: it preserves the highest average fidelity while also exhibiting the smallest shot-to-shot fluctuations. This is precisely the setting in which the RL formulation becomes most compelling, since the policy is no longer judged only by how well it fits one deterministic control trajectory, but by how reliably it performs under a physically relevant family of perturbations.

The comparison with the Adam baseline reinforces this interpretation. Adam remains a meaningful and nontrivial reference, and its performance confirms that direct gradient-based optimization can still produce strong controls in this multilevel setting. However, the present results indicate that the best robust operating point is no longer attained by the direct optimizer once stochastic perturbations are taken into account. Instead, the noise-optimized TRPO controller dominates Adam in both of the robustness metrics considered here: it maintains a higher mean fidelity under noise and, at the same time, suppresses the variance more effectively. This is a particularly strong result, because it shows that the stochastic RL controller does not improve one aspect of robustness at the expense of another. Rather, it improves both the \emph{accuracy} and the \emph{stability} of the noisy implementation.

From a control-theoretic perspective, this suggests that the policy trained in the noisy environment has internalized pulse strategies that are intrinsically less sensitive to random perturbations of the control channels and of the anharmonicity-related Hamiltonian parameter. In other words, the learned policy appears to avoid control trajectories that are excessively fragile to small stochastic deviations, and instead converges toward solutions that remain effective across a broader ensemble of realizations. This interpretation is fully consistent with the original logic of the UFO framework, where robustness is not treated as a post hoc diagnostic, but as a property to be incorporated directly into the optimization environment \cite{Niu2019}.

A further important consequence is that robustness should be understood here as a genuinely multidimensional notion. The average fidelity and the fidelity variance do not probe the same aspect of performance, and neither of them is sufficient on its own. A practically useful controller should remain both accurate \emph{and} statistically stable under repeated noisy executions. The present results show that the noise-optimized TRPO controller is the only method among the three considered that consistently occupies the most favorable region with respect to both criteria. For this reason, the evidence presented in this subsection supports a stronger conclusion than a simple ranking of optimizers: it supports the claim that exposure to the appropriate stochastic environment during training changes the qualitative character of the learned control policy.

For the broader structure of the chapter, this subsection therefore plays a decisive interpretive role. The single-target nominal benchmark established that all methods can produce meaningful high-quality analog controls, but did not by itself justify the additional machinery of noisy-environment reinforcement learning. The robustness study does. It shows that, once the control problem is evaluated in the physically relevant regime of stochastic actuation errors, the decisive advantage of the UFO-inspired RL framework lies not merely in pulse optimization, but in the ability to \emph{learn robustness} directly from the training environment. In this sense, the present reproduction supports one of the main scientific messages of the original paper: robustness against stochastic control errors is not an accidental emergent feature of analog control, but a trainable property that can be significantly improved by embedding the relevant perturbation model into the optimization loop \cite{Niu2019}.

\subsection{Runtime sweep protocol}
\label{subsec:runtime_sweep_protocol}

To study hardware efficiency beyond the single-target setting, the analysis is extended from the representative gate
\(N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2)\) to the full one-parameter family
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{target}}(\alpha)
=
N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2),
\qquad
\alpha \in [0,\pi].
\label{eq:runtime_target_family}
\end{equation}
The purpose of this subsection is therefore different from that of the robustness study in
section \ref{subsec:noise_model}--\ref{subsec:robustness_interpretation}. There, the central question was whether
stochastic training improves the stability of the learned controller under noisy perturbations. Here, by contrast,
the relevant question is whether direct analog control can realize different gates in the family
\(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\) with runtimes that are competitive with, or even shorter than, those associated with
standard discrete gate synthesis. This makes the present sweep a family-level efficiency test rather than a
single-target robustness benchmark, in direct continuity with one of the two main empirical claims of the original
UFO paper \cite{Niu2019}.
% :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

The primary runtime experiment is implemented as a \emph{curriculum TRPO sweep}. More precisely, for fixed
\(\gamma=\pi/2\), the controller is trained sequentially over increasing values of \(\alpha\), using the best
checkpoint obtained at one value of \(\alpha\) as the initialization for the next one. 
\footnote{The parameter choice is in accordance with Section \ref{subsec:simulation_and_training_parameters_choice}.} 
Operationally, this means that the runtime sweep is not performed by retraining each target completely
from scratch, but by exploiting continuity in target space: nearby gates in the family are assumed to admit
partially transferable control representations, so that the policy learned for one \(\alpha\) provides a useful
warm start for the next one. This choice is strongly aligned with the policy-based logic of the framework, since
TRPO is specifically designed to perform stable updates of continuous stochastic policies under a KL-constrained
trust-region step \cite{Schulman2015}.
% :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Alongside this curriculum-based RL sweep, a second experiment is considered. The Adam baseline curriculum already discussed in \ref{subsec:adam_baseline_results} in order to
probe the same gate family without transfer learning. In that case, each target is optimized independently from
scratch, and the best runtime is selected through a horizon sweep over a discrete set of candidate durations. The role of this second sweep is methodologically useful: it separates
the effect of analog controllability across the gate family from the additional benefit that may arise from
curriculum transfer. In other words, the Adam sweep provides a family-level baseline for how
homogeneous or inhomogeneous the analog optimization problem already is, whereas the curriculum TRPO sweep tests
whether neighboring gates can be traversed more efficiently by reusing previously learned control structure.

The final ingredient of the protocol is the comparison with the optimal discrete gate-synthesis baseline discussed
earlier in Sec.~\ref{subsec:baselines_comparison_strategy}. Following the benchmark logic of the reference paper,
the relevant reference time is the estimated synthesized runtime
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{syn}} \approx 215\,\mathrm{ns},
\label{eq:runtime_synthesis_baseline_recalled}
\end{equation}
obtained from the optimal decomposition of \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)\) into single-qubit rotations and entangling
gates, together with state-of-the-art gate-time estimates for those primitives \cite{Niu2019}. The runtime curves
reported below should therefore be interpreted against this fixed hardware-efficiency reference: for each
\(\alpha\), the central quantity of interest is whether the optimized analog controller lies below, near, or above
\(T_{\mathrm{syn}}\). This makes the sweep directly relevant to the broader physical question motivating the whole
section, namely whether direct analog control on the gmon can exploit the native interaction structure of the
device to outperform standard compiled implementations over a nontrivial family of two-qubit targets.

Figure~\ref{fig:runtime_vs_alpha_curriculum} reveals that the runtime profile along the family
\(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\) is highly structured rather than monotonic. Instead of a smooth increase or decrease
with \(\alpha\), the curriculum sweep exhibits a sequence of distinct regimes: an initial bootstrap region at very
small \(\alpha\), a broad central basin of comparatively fast solutions, a sharp minimum at
\(\alpha \approx \pi/2\), and a final degradation region as \(\alpha\) approaches \(\pi\). This already suggests
that the time required to implement the target gate is strongly dependent on how favorably the target aligns with
the native controllable dynamics of the gmon system, rather than being set by a uniform compilation overhead across
the whole family.

A first important point is that the \(\alpha=0\) phase should be interpreted separately from the rest of the
curve. In the present curriculum protocol, this is the only point that is not warm-started from a previous nearby
target, and therefore it acts as a genuine cold-start initialization of the whole sweep. Consistently with that
role, it is also the weakest point of the experiment: the final deterministic fidelity remains only
\(F \approx 0.558\), the runtime saturates at \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\), the leakage is still
\(4.8\times 10^{-3}\), and the corresponding \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\) average fidelity is only about \(0.613\).
For this reason, \(\alpha=0\) is operationally informative, but it should not be overinterpreted as representative
of the family-level runtime trend. The more meaningful structure of the curve emerges once the curriculum has
entered its transferable regime, starting already from \(\alpha \approx 0.157\).

Beyond this bootstrap point, the curve shows that the family is not uniformly difficult. A first reasonably good
region appears already for \(\alpha \approx 0.157\) and \(0.314\), where the controller reaches runtimes of
\(142\,\mathrm{ns}\) and \(140\,\mathrm{ns}\), with nominal fidelities above \(0.988\) and robust average
fidelities around \(0.951\) and \(0.953\), respectively. This indicates that the curriculum begins to exploit
cross-target continuity quite early. However, the next interval, roughly
\(\alpha \in [0.47,0.79]\), becomes harder again: the final runtime returns to the maximum observed value of
\(180\,\mathrm{ns}\), while the final fidelities drop to the \(0.948\)--\(0.954\) range and the
\(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\) average fidelity falls to about \(0.906\)--\(0.918\). This is not merely a runtime
effect: the same region also exhibits visibly larger leakage and boundary-cost levels than the fast central basin,
which suggests that the controller is using less clean pulse shapes and paying a higher dynamical price to reach
the target.

The most striking feature of the figure is the central runtime valley around
\(\alpha \in [1.1,2.4]\), where the controller becomes substantially faster while maintaining very high quality.
In this interval, several targets are solved in the \(76\)--\(140\,\mathrm{ns}\) range, and the best part of the
basin lies roughly between \(\alpha \approx 1.884\) and \(\alpha \approx 2.355\), where the observed runtimes are
\(108\,\mathrm{ns}\), \(106\,\mathrm{ns}\), \(110\,\mathrm{ns}\), and \(118\,\mathrm{ns}\), all with nominal
fidelity above \(0.982\) and robust average fidelity at \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\) still around
\(0.954\)--\(0.959\). This is a genuinely important result: the runtime reduction is not obtained by sacrificing
accuracy, since the same part of the curve also remains in the high-fidelity and high-robustness regime. The curve
therefore indicates a broad family region in which fast analog implementations are not isolated accidents, but
repeatable solutions.

At the center of the family, the point \(\alpha=\pi/2\) stands out as an extreme minimum. Here the controller
terminates after only \(2\,\mathrm{ns}\), with essentially unit nominal fidelity
(\(F \approx 0.999999\)), negligible leakage (\(\sim 4\times 10^{-10}\)), extremely small boundary cost, and
average noisy fidelity still equal to \(0.9994\) at \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\). In addition, the corresponding
phase requires only a single curriculum iteration, making it not only the fastest gate in the sweep but also the
easiest one to learn. This is not a generic numerical fluctuation: it is a singular point in the empirical
landscape, and the sharpness of the dip strongly suggests a special dynamical compatibility between the target gate
at \(\alpha=\pi/2\) and the native interaction structure of the device. The physical interpretation of this point
will be discussed in detail in Paragraph~4, but already at the level of the curve it is clear that the runtime
landscape contains a privileged native-alignment point rather than a smooth continuum of equally hard targets.

After this central minimum, the runtime rises again but not immediately catastrophically. The interval
\(\alpha \in [1.727,2.669]\) still forms a favorable region: runtimes remain between \(124\,\mathrm{ns}\) and
\(144\,\mathrm{ns}\), fidelities stay in the \(0.982\)--\(0.992\) range, and robustness remains high. Only when
the sweep enters the final high-\(\alpha\) edge, roughly \(\alpha \gtrsim 2.826\), does the curve degrade more
clearly. In that last region, the runtime saturates again at \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\), while nominal fidelity falls
to \(0.922\)--\(0.940\) and robust average fidelity to \(0.887\)--\(0.902\). This suggests that the favorable
transfer structure exploited by the curriculum over the central basin becomes progressively weaker as the target is
pushed toward the far end of the family.

A further nontrivial insight emerges when the final runtime is compared with the
\emph{minimum-cost time} reached within the same phase. For many of the well-converged points, the two coincide,
which indicates that the controller not only discovers a good time scale but also stabilizes it as the final
solution. However, in several difficult regions this coincidence breaks down. For example, around
\(\alpha \approx 0.471\) and \(0.628\), the final runtime is \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\) even though the lowest-cost
intermediate trajectory is reached already around \(142\)--\(146\,\mathrm{ns}\); similarly, near
\(\alpha \approx 2.826\), \(2.983\), and \(\pi\), the minimum-cost times lie around \(152\)--\(158\,\mathrm{ns}\)
while the final rollout still ends at \(180\,\mathrm{ns}\). This indicates that in the hard regions the agent can
transiently visit faster candidate solutions, but does not consistently stabilize them under the full optimization
criterion. In other words, the difficult parts of the runtime curve are not simply regions where short solutions do
not exist at all; rather, they are regions where short solutions appear to be less stable, less robust, or harder
to preserve throughout training.

The same reading is supported by the auxiliary diagnostics shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:runtime_vs_alpha_curriculum}. The fastest phases are
also the ones with the cleanest constraint profile: lower leakage, lower boundary cost, and, in general, better
agreement between nominal and noisy fidelity. Conversely, the slowest phases are precisely those where leakage and
boundary penalties rise again. This coupling between runtime and constraint satisfaction is crucial, because it
shows that the runtime curve is not an isolated geometric observable; it is embedded in the same control trade-off
already discussed for the single-target case. Short runtimes are achieved when the controller can simultaneously
realize the target, suppress leakage, and keep the pulse endpoints under control. Long runtimes reappear when one
or more of these constraints become harder to satisfy at the same time.

Overall, the runtime curve therefore conveys a richer message than a simple pointwise comparison of gate durations.
It shows that the family \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\) contains a broad favorable analog-control basin, centered
around a particularly privileged point at \(\alpha=\pi/2\), but also flanked by regions where the controller
either needs longer trajectories or fails to stabilize the shorter ones it transiently discovers. This makes the
curve especially informative for the rest of the subsection: it is not only a record of achieved runtimes, but a
map of where the analog-control landscape is intrinsically easy, where it is fragile, and where the curriculum
transfer mechanism is most and least effective.

\subsection{Comparison with gate synthesis}
\label{subsec:comparison_gate_synthesis}

A central question raised by the runtime sweep is whether the analog-control solutions found by the curriculum-trained
TRPO agent are merely competitive with standard discrete compilation, or whether they actually outperform it in a
meaningful way. To answer this, the natural reference is the optimal gate-synthesis decomposition of the family
\(N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)\), discussed in the original UFO paper on the basis of the Vatan--Williams two-qubit
synthesis result \cite{VatanWilliams2004,Niu2019}. In that benchmark, the target unitary is decomposed into a
minimal-depth circuit over arbitrary one-qubit rotations and entangling gates, yielding an estimated runtime of
approximately \(215\,\mathrm{ns}\) for the \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\gamma)\) family under the hardware timing assumptions
adopted in \cite{Niu2019}. Although this is not a pulse-level optimization in the exact same analog-control space
used in the present work, it is still the correct compilation baseline for assessing whether direct continuous
control provides a genuine temporal advantage over standard discrete synthesis.

Under that reference, the present runtime sweep shows that the analog-control strategy is not just occasionally
better: across essentially the entire nontrivial family \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\), it remains below the
gate-synthesis benchmark. Even the difficult edge points of the sweep, where the learned controller saturates at
\(180\,\mathrm{ns}\), still remain faster than the synthesis estimate by about \(35\,\mathrm{ns}\), i.e. by roughly
\(16\%\). This already matters, because those are precisely the hardest regions of the landscape, where nominal
fidelity and noisy fidelity both deteriorate and where the curriculum no longer finds the clean short-time solutions
available in the central basin. In other words, the analog approach preserves a runtime advantage even where the
optimization problem becomes visibly more difficult.

The advantage becomes much more substantial in the broad central part of the family. Around
\(\alpha \in [1.73,2.36]\), the final learned runtimes lie between \(106\,\mathrm{ns}\) and \(124\,\mathrm{ns}\),
which corresponds to a reduction of about \(42\%\) to \(51\%\) relative to the \(215\,\mathrm{ns}\) gate-synthesis
reference. In this regime, the speedup is therefore not marginal but systematic. Importantly, this reduction does
not come with a collapse in control quality: the same points remain in the high-fidelity regime
(\(F \approx 0.982\)--\(0.986\)) and maintain robust average fidelity near \(0.955\)--\(0.959\) at
\(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\). The comparison is therefore qualitatively stronger than a bare runtime statement:
for a substantial subset of the gate family, analog control is simultaneously faster, accurate, and reasonably
robust, rather than merely trading correctness for speed.

An even stronger discrepancy appears at selected favorable points of the landscape. For instance, at
\(\alpha \approx 1.10\) the learned runtime drops to \(76\,\mathrm{ns}\), corresponding to a speedup of almost a
factor \(3\) over the synthesis baseline, while still retaining nominal fidelity above \(0.98\) and robust average
fidelity above \(0.96\). The singular point \(\alpha=\pi/2\) is more extreme still: the controller terminates after
\(2\,\mathrm{ns}\), with essentially unit nominal fidelity and negligible leakage. This value should not be treated
as representative of the entire family, because it reflects a very special alignment between target and native
Hamiltonian structure; nevertheless, it is conceptually important. It demonstrates that, at least for some targets,
the compilation overhead implicit in discrete gate synthesis is not a fundamental lower bound on execution time.
Rather, it is an artifact of expressing the target through a universal discrete gate alphabet instead of exploiting
the analog structure of the hardware directly.

This distinction is crucial for interpreting the result correctly. The discrete synthesis benchmark is "optimal"
within a circuit model based on a prescribed elementary gate library, whereas the analog controller optimizes
directly in the continuous Hamiltonian-control space. The two are therefore solving related but not identical
optimization problems. For this reason, the present comparison should not be read as a statement that the analog
controller has beaten an absolute quantum speed limit. What it does show, however, is that once the hardware admits
a rich native interaction and sufficiently flexible pulse shaping, the shortest experimentally meaningful
implementation of a target gate need not coincide with its shortest decomposition over a discrete universal gate set.
From the point of view of near-term hardware efficiency, this is precisely the relevant notion of improvement.

The comparison also clarifies where the gain is modest and where it is dramatic. In the hard regions near the two
edges of the sweep, the analog controller is only moderately faster than gate synthesis, and the runtime gap narrows
precisely when leakage, boundary penalties, and robustness degradation become more pronounced. In the central basin,
by contrast, the gap widens sharply. This indicates that the benefit of analog control is strongly target-dependent:
it is largest when the target gate can be realized in a way that is geometrically and dynamically aligned with the
native couplings of the physical device, and smallest when extra control resources are needed to compensate for a
less favorable target structure. Thus, the runtime comparison is not only a numerical benchmark, but also a
diagnostic of how much compilation overhead can be removed by working directly at the Hamiltonian level.

Overall, the results support the same qualitative conclusion reported in the original UFO work, but in a more
fine-grained family-resolved form. The analog-control solutions found here are broadly faster than the standard
gate-synthesis reference, often by a large margin, and the strongest gains occur exactly in the regions where the
control landscape appears most compatible with the underlying gmon interaction. This confirms that the runtime
advantage of analog control is real, not anecdotal: for this gate family, direct pulse-level optimization can indeed
surpass standard discrete synthesis as a practically relevant notion of implementation efficiency.

\subsection{Physical interpretation}
\label{subsec:physical_interpretation}

The runtime landscape observed in Fig.~\ref{fig:runtime_vs_alpha_curriculum} is physically meaningful, and should not be read as a purely numerical peculiarity of the optimizer. The key point is that the target family considered here,
\(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\), is not arbitrary with respect to the underlying gmon Hamiltonian. As discussed in the system-model section, the native entangling interaction is generated primarily by the exchange-like coupling term
\(g(t)\,(\hat a_2^\dagger \hat a_1+\hat a_1^\dagger \hat a_2)\), whose effective action on the computational subspace is naturally aligned with the
\(XX+YY\) sector of the two-qubit interaction. Consequently, when the desired gate requires a balance of nonlocal content that can be synthesized largely along this native direction, the controller does not need to "fight" the hardware: it can exploit the intrinsic entangling channel directly, while using the remaining controls mainly to shape phases, suppress leakage, and satisfy the boundary constraints. In precisely this regime, shorter runtimes are not only empirically observed, but physically expected. This is fully consistent with the original motivation of the UFO framework, which emphasizes that analog control becomes especially advantageous when the target gate is geometrically close to the entangling structure naturally generated by the device Hamiltonian \cite{Niu2019}.

This interpretation also explains why the best-performing region of the sweep is concentrated in the middle of the
\(\alpha\)-interval rather than at its boundaries. In the central basin, the optimizer can realize the required nonlocal action with relatively modest auxiliary control effort: the pulse does not need large corrective excursions in the local channels, the boundary term remains moderate, and the TSWT leakage proxy stays under control. From a dynamical viewpoint, the gate can be implemented by letting the system evolve along a direction that is already available in hardware, rather than by repeatedly compensating unwanted motion through more elaborate control shaping. The shorter runtimes found in this region are therefore plausibly associated with smoother and more "natural" trajectories in control space. This interpretation is reinforced by the fact that the same \(\alpha\)-window also displays strong nominal fidelities and good noisy fidelities at \(\sigma=1\,\mathrm{MHz}\): the region that is faster is also the region that is easier to control reliably. In that sense, temporal efficiency and robustness are not independent observations here, but two manifestations of the same underlying alignment between target geometry and native device physics.

By contrast, the growth of runtime toward the two edges of the family is also physically interpretable. As \(\alpha\) moves away from the favorable central window, the desired gate becomes less directly compatible with the entangling evolution that the gmon hardware provides most naturally. The controller must then rely more heavily on additional detuning and microwave components to steer the dynamics toward the target while simultaneously suppressing population transfer outside the computational subspace. This extra compensation effort has two immediate consequences. First, it tends to lengthen the control sequence, because more time is needed to accumulate the correct effective nonlocal action without violating the leakage and boundary penalties. Second, it makes the solution more fragile, since longer and more structured pulses expose the system for a greater duration to both coherent modeling imperfections and stochastic perturbations. The qualitative correlation observed across the sweep (higher runtimes, slightly worse robust fidelities, and reduced margin to the gate-synthesis baseline near the edges) is therefore exactly what one would expect if those target gates were dynamically less aligned with the native Hamiltonian.

The special behavior near \(\alpha=\pi/2\) should be interpreted in the same spirit, but with additional caution. The extremely short runtime found there suggests that this point may correspond to a particularly favorable symmetry or local-equivalence configuration within the gate family, so that the desired transformation can be reached with very little pulse duration once the controller is placed in the appropriate dynamical sector. This does not mean that \(2\,\mathrm{ns}\) should be taken as a generic realizability statement for arbitrary two-qubit gates, nor that the analog controller has saturated a universal quantum speed limit. Rather, it indicates that within a restricted family and on a hardware platform with a strongly structured native interaction, isolated gates may exist whose implementation cost collapses because the target is exceptionally well matched to the underlying entangling generator. For the purposes of the present thesis, the important conclusion is therefore not the extremal value itself, but the broader structural pattern around it: the runtime minima occur where physical compatibility is highest.

Taken together, these observations support the main conceptual message of this subsection. The runtime advantage of analog control over discrete synthesis is largest not in a random subset of the target family, but precisely where the target gate is best aligned with the natural interaction channels of the superconducting device. This makes the result much stronger than a purely algorithmic benchmark. It shows that the improvement is rooted in the physics of the platform: direct pulse-level optimization can remove part of the compilation overhead associated with universal gate decomposition because it exploits the Hamiltonian that the hardware already implements natively. Conversely, when that alignment deteriorates, the runtime advantage shrinks, the controller must work harder to enforce fidelity and leakage constraints, and the solutions become less efficient. The runtime sweep therefore provides a concrete dynamical interpretation of why analog control can outperform standard gate synthesis on this family: it is not merely optimizing faster, but optimizing along the natural directions of the device itself.

\section{Overall assessment of the validated framework}
\label{sec:overall_assessment}

\subsection{Summary of empirical evidence}
\label{subsec:summary_empirical_evidence}
\subsection{Summary of empirical evidence}
\label{subsec:summary_empirical_evidence}

The empirical results presented in this chapter support a coherent overall assessment of the validated control framework. First, the single-target study on the representative gate \(N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2)\) shows that the present implementation is able to produce high-quality analog controls in the full multilevel setting, both through direct pulse optimization and through reinforcement learning. In the purely nominal regime, the Adam baseline emerges as the strongest method on this benchmark, achieving the most favorable balance among fidelity, leakage, runtime, and total cost. This point is methodologically important, because it shows that the RL-based controller is not being compared against a weak reference, but against a meaningful direct-optimization baseline that already performs very well on the deterministic problem.

At the same time, the robustness study makes clear that the main strength of the framework does not lie in nominal optimization alone, but in its behavior under stochastic perturbations. Once Gaussian control noise is introduced at evaluation time, the TRPO controller trained directly in the noisy environment becomes the strongest overall solution among the considered methods. More precisely, it achieves both the highest average fidelity and the lowest fidelity variance across the explored range of noise strengths, thereby outperforming both the nominal TRPO controller and the Adam baseline. This is one of the central empirical findings of the chapter, since it indicates that robustness is not merely assessed \emph{a posteriori}, but can be effectively learned through stochastic training, in direct agreement with the main physical intuition of the UFO framework \cite{Niu2019}.

A second major line of evidence concerns runtime and hardware efficiency. The family-level analysis on the submanifold \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\) shows that the runtime of the optimized analog controls is highly structured across target space, with a broad favorable region in which the learned solutions remain well below the standard optimal gate-synthesis reference. In particular, the strongest reductions are observed in the central part of the family, where the target gate is more naturally aligned with the native interaction structure of the gmon Hamiltonian. This confirms that the runtime advantage of analog control is not a uniform property of all targets, but a physically structured effect that depends on how well the desired nonlocal transformation matches the controllable dynamics of the device.

The parameter-choice studies further reinforce the internal consistency of this picture. The neural-network architecture sweep showed that performance does depend measurably on the actor--critic capacity, but not in a monotonic way: larger or deeper networks do not systematically improve the final controller. In particular, the wider \(64\text{-}64\text{-}64\) configuration achieved the best aggregate performance under the reduced-budget sweep, while the paper architecture \(64\text{-}32\text{-}32\) remained extremely close in overall quality and was therefore retained for the main experiments as the most natural paper-faithful choice. Likewise, the sensitivity study on the UFO coefficients showed that the results are physically interpretable but reasonably stable around the reference scalarization: too small a fidelity weight clearly harms control quality, whereas an excessively strong boundary penalty can reduce leakage at the price of destabilizing the broader fidelity--robustness trade-off. Within this picture, the original weight choice \((\chi,\beta,\mu,\kappa)=(10,10,0.2,0.1)\) remained near the best trade-off region in the present implementation and was therefore adopted throughout the remainder of the chapter.

Taken together, these results support a precise interpretation of the validated framework. The reproduced control pipeline does not suggest that reinforcement learning is universally superior to direct gradient-based pulse optimization in every regime. Rather, it shows that the RL-based approach becomes especially compelling when the control problem is treated as a genuinely multi-objective and noise-aware task, where fidelity, leakage, runtime, and robustness must be balanced simultaneously. In this sense, the empirical evidence supports the view that the main value of the UFO framework lies in providing a unified and physically meaningful strategy for robust analog quantum control, rather than in offering a universally dominant nominal optimizer.

\subsection{Agreement and discrepancies with the original paper}
\label{subsec:agreement_discrepancies_original_paper}

The empirical evidence discussed in this chapter shows a substantial qualitative agreement with the main scientific claims of the original work by Niu \emph{et al.} \cite{Niu2019}, while also revealing a number of quantitatively relevant discrepancies that are important for a balanced interpretation of the present reproduction. The strongest point of agreement concerns the role of stochastic training. In the present implementation, the controller trained directly in the noisy environment systematically outperforms both the nominal TRPO controller and the direct Adam baseline under noisy evaluation, achieving higher average fidelity and lower fidelity variance across the explored range of Gaussian perturbation strengths. This result is fully consistent with the core idea of the original paper, namely that robustness against stochastic control errors should not be treated merely as a post-training diagnostic, but as a property that can be actively learned by embedding the relevant perturbation model into the optimization loop.

A second important point of agreement concerns the runtime analysis on the family \(N(\alpha,\alpha,\pi/2)\). Also in the present study, the analog-control solutions are found to be broadly faster than the standard gate-synthesis reference, often by a large margin, and the strongest gains are observed in the regions where the target gate appears most naturally aligned with the native entangling structure of the gmon Hamiltonian. This is again in line with the physical interpretation emphasized in the original paper: the main runtime advantage of analog control does not arise from a generic numerical improvement alone, but from the possibility of exploiting hardware-native interactions more directly than is possible in a compiled circuit decomposition \cite{Niu2019}. In this sense, the present reproduction supports not only the runtime trend itself, but also the physical mechanism proposed to explain it.

At the same time, the present results do not amount to a numerically exact reproduction of all aspects of the original study. The clearest discrepancy emerges in the nominal single-target benchmark. In the present implementation, the direct Adam baseline remains superior to the nominal TRPO controller on the representative gate \(N(2.2,2.2,\pi/2)\), achieving a better total cost, a shorter runtime, and a lower leakage estimate, while also retaining slightly higher nominal fidelity. This suggests that, at least in the deterministic regime and on this particular target, the RL-based controller does not exhibit an intrinsic nominal advantage over a strong direct pulse optimizer. Such a result does not invalidate the original framework, but it does indicate that the main empirical strength of the method is more clearly visible in the robust-control setting than in the purely nominal one.

A further discrepancy concerns the uniformity of the runtime advantage across the gate family. While the original paper reports very strong runtime gains and emphasizes the existence of highly favorable analog realizations, the present family-level sweep reveals a more structured and non-uniform landscape. Alongside a broad central basin of fast and accurate solutions, there also appear harder regions near the edges of the explored \(\alpha\)-interval, where the learned controller either saturates at the maximum allowed runtime or fails to stabilize the shorter intermediate solutions that it transiently visits during training. This indicates that the analog advantage is real, but not equally strong everywhere, and that the control landscape may contain more pronounced inhomogeneities than a simplified reading of the original results might suggest.

These discrepancies are scientifically unsurprising and, in fact, methodologically healthy. The present study is based on an independent implementation, with its own discretization choices, filtering pipeline, stopping rules, training budgets, and evaluation protocol. Even when the physical framework is matched as closely as possible to that of the original paper, strict numerical coincidence should not be expected in such a high-dimensional and non-convex control problem. Moreover, the results obtained here suggest that some quantitative features of the framework may be sensitive to choices that are not conceptually central, but still practically influential, such as the precise training horizon, the curriculum schedule, the network architecture, or the scalarization balance induced by the UFO weights.

The parameter studies reported in this chapter support exactly this interpretation. The architecture sweep showed that the quantitative performance of the RL controller does depend on the policy/value network size, but not in a simple monotonic fashion: wider or deeper models do not automatically produce better control solutions, and the paper architecture \(64\text{-}32\text{-}32\) remains highly competitive despite not being the best aggregate performer in the reduced-budget sweep. Similarly, the sensitivity analysis of the UFO coefficients showed that the framework is reasonably stable around the original scalarization, while still displaying interpretable trade-offs when the fidelity or boundary penalties are reweighted too aggressively. These results do not undermine the qualitative comparison with the original study; rather, they help explain why exact numerical agreement is not the appropriate standard of success in an independent reproduction of this kind.

For this reason, the present reproduction should be regarded as successful primarily at the level of the main qualitative claims rather than at the level of exact numerical duplication. The core conceptual picture advanced in the original paper is clearly supported: noisy-environment RL improves robustness, analog control can outperform standard gate synthesis in runtime, and the strongest gains emerge when the target gate is well aligned with the native interaction geometry of the device. At the same time, the thesis also refines that picture by showing more explicitly that nominal superiority is not automatic, that family-level performance is structurally non-uniform, and that the practical behavior of the framework must be interpreted in light of optimization difficulty and implementation-dependent trade-offs. In this sense, the relation between the present study and the original paper is not one of simple confirmation or contradiction, but rather one of substantial qualitative validation combined with a more granular and critical quantitative assessment.
