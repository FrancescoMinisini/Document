\noindent
This thesis studies a recent method for controlling superconducting qubits, one of the main physical platforms used in quantum computing. In practice, making a quantum computer work is not only a matter of designing algorithms: it is also necessary to drive a physical device in such a way that it performs the desired quantum operation accurately, quickly, and reliably. This is a difficult task, because real quantum hardware is affected by noise, experimental imperfections, and unwanted transitions outside the ideal logical states. The thesis investigates whether a reinforcement-learning-based approach proposed in the recent literature can offer a scientifically meaningful and practically useful way to address this problem.

The work had two main objectives. The first was to reconstruct as faithfully as possible the framework introduced by \emph{Niu, Boixo, Smelyanskiy, and Neven (2019)}, starting from its physical model, its optimization strategy, and its main numerical claims. The second was to test this framework critically through an independent empirical analysis. In other words, the thesis was not limited to summarizing the original paper, but aimed to verify how well the method works, under which conditions it performs well, and what its main limitations are.

A central idea of the thesis is that quantum control on realistic hardware is a multi-objective problem. A good control strategy must achieve several goals at the same time: it must implement the desired quantum gate with high accuracy, keep the operation short in time, reduce unwanted population transfer to non-computational states, and remain stable in the presence of noise affecting the control signals. For this reason, the thesis first develops the physical and methodological background needed to frame the problem correctly. In particular, it explains why superconducting qubits cannot be treated as perfectly ideal two-level systems, why unwanted transitions are a serious issue, and why robustness against noise must be considered from the beginning rather than only after an apparently good solution has been found.

After this general introduction, the thesis reconstructs the specific ingredients of the reference framework in a more explicit and organized way. These include a model of two coupled superconducting qubits, an analytical method to estimate unwanted leakage outside the logical subspace, a cost function that combines gate accuracy, leakage suppression, smooth control behavior, and runtime, and a reinforcement-learning formulation in which the controller learns step by step how to generate suitable control pulses in a noisy environment. This reconstruction was implemented within a coherent simulation pipeline, so that different optimization strategies could be compared under the same physical assumptions.

The empirical results lead to a nuanced but clear conclusion. On a representative benchmark with no noise during evaluation, a direct gradient-based optimization method performed better than the reinforcement-learning controller. This shows that reinforcement learning is not automatically the best choice for every quantum-control problem. However, when the analysis was extended to noisy conditions, the controller trained directly in a stochastic environment became the strongest overall method, achieving both higher average performance and smaller variability under perturbations. This supports one of the main messages of the original paper: if realistic sources of noise are already included during training, the learned controller can become significantly more robust.

The thesis also examined how the method behaves when some important parameters are changed. In particular, it studied the effect of different neural-network sizes and different choices for the weights entering the cost function. These analyses showed that the performance of the method does depend on these design choices, but not in a simple or purely monotonic way: larger models do not automatically perform better, and the balance between accuracy, speed, and leakage control has a clear physical meaning. This part of the work is important because it shows that the main conclusions of the thesis do not rest on arbitrary parameter tuning.

Another relevant result concerns execution time. For a broad family of two-qubit target operations, the study shows that direct analog control can remain substantially faster than implementing the same operations through standard decompositions into elementary gates. This advantage is especially visible when the target operation is naturally compatible with the physical interactions already present in the hardware. In this sense, the thesis highlights not only the role of machine learning, but also a more general physical idea: on near-term quantum devices, it can be advantageous to exploit the native behavior of the hardware as directly as possible.

Overall, the thesis does not claim that reinforcement learning provides a universally superior solution to quantum control. Rather, it shows that the framework studied here is valuable because it offers a unified and physically motivated way to balance several competing requirements that are all important in realistic quantum devices. The main contribution of the work is therefore twofold: on the one hand, it provides a structured reconstruction of a recent and influential control framework; on the other hand, it offers an independent assessment of its strengths, limitations, and practical significance for robust quantum gate design on superconducting hardware.
